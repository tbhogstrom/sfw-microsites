## Chimney Chase Repair and Rebuilding
Chimney chases in the Pacific Northwest are constantly exposed to rain and moisture, which can lead to structural issues over time. Repairing and rebuilding a chimney chase involves inspecting and addressing various components, including the mortar joints, flashing, and chimney cap. Ensuring that the mortar joints are solid is crucial, as crumbly joints can lead to significant structural problems if left unattended<sup>1</sup>. Additionally, proper flashing must be installed to prevent moisture from penetrating the framing and causing wood rot<sup>2</sup>. A sloping mortar chimney cap or an overhanging concrete crown can effectively shed water and protect the chimney’s integrity. If the chimney cap is cracked or missing, it's a sign that water may be entering the structure. Repairing these elements promptly is essential to maintaining a safe and sound chimney chase. For Portland homeowners, addressing these repairs can prevent costly damages and ensure their chimney system functions efficiently. If your chimney chase is showing signs of wear, it's time to take action and restore it to its optimal condition.

## Chimney Chase Structural Framing Repair
When dealing with chimney chase structural framing issues, it's crucial to address any water damage promptly. The Pacific Northwest's abundant rainfall can lead to rotted wood within the chimney chase, compromising the structural integrity of your chimney<sup>3</sup>. To ensure long-lasting repairs, we remove any damaged framing and replace it with new, treated lumber that can withstand moisture exposure. This process often involves carefully dismantling sections of the chimney chase to access and replace the compromised framing<sup>4</sup>. Additionally, installing proper flashing is essential to prevent future water infiltration, as it acts as a barrier against rainwater penetrating the chase structure<sup>3</sup>. If your chimney chase exhibits signs of structural damage, such as leaning or visible gaps, it's time to take action. Our experienced team is ready to restore your chimney chase's integrity, ensuring your home remains safe and secure.

## Chimney Chase Reframing & Reinforcement
Reframing and reinforcing a chimney chase is crucial for maintaining structural integrity, especially in the Pacific Northwest where moisture can be a persistent issue. Over time, water infiltration can weaken the wooden framing of a chimney chase, leading to potential safety hazards. Proper reframing involves removing any rotted or damaged wood and replacing it with new, pressure-treated lumber to withstand the damp climate. Reinforcement may be necessary to ensure that the structure can support the weight of the chimney components and resist the forces of wind and rain. Utilizing a combination of metal braces and screws can add additional strength and stability to the frame<sup>5</sup>. It’s also important to ensure that the flashing and counterflashing are properly installed to prevent water from seeping into the structure<sup>6</sup>. If your chimney chase is showing signs of wear, don’t wait for further damage to occur; schedule a professional inspection to discuss reframing and reinforcement options.

## Wooden Chimney Chase Framing Repair
Wooden chimney chases are vulnerable to the Pacific Northwest's notorious rain and moisture, leading to potential wood rot and structural damage. Over time, water can infiltrate through compromised flashing or damaged siding, causing the wooden framing to weaken and deteriorate. Regular inspections are essential to detect early signs of damage such as warping, mold, or soft spots in the wood. When addressing repairs, it’s crucial to remove all affected wood and replace it with pressure-treated lumber to ensure long-lasting resilience against moisture<sup>7</sup>. Proper sealing and flashing are vital to protect the new framing from future water exposure<sup>8</sup>. By reinforcing your chimney chase with durable materials and techniques, you can prevent costly repairs down the line and maintain the structural integrity of your home. If you suspect your wooden chimney chase needs repair, schedule an inspection to safeguard your home against the elements.

## Chimney Chase Top Rebuild
Rebuilding the chimney chase top is crucial to protecting your home from the Pacific Northwest's relentless rain and moisture. A well-constructed chase top acts as a shield, preventing water from penetrating the chimney and causing structural damage. There are two main types of crowns you can consider: a sloping mortar crown and an overhanging concrete crown. In areas with heavy precipitation, like Portland, an in-place concrete crown is recommended. This crown overhangs the chimney by 1 to 1½ inches, effectively acting as a drip edge to keep rainwater off the bricks and away from the chimney structure<sup>9</sup>. The construction process involves building a frame from 2x2s that snugly fits over the chimney top, ensuring a precise overhang<sup>10</sup>. This method not only enhances durability but also prolongs the lifespan of the chimney by minimizing water-related deterioration. If your chimney chase top has seen better days, consider rebuilding it with a robust overhanging crown to protect your home from the elements.

## Chimney Chase Framing Replacement
Replacing the framing of a chimney chase is a critical task for Portland homeowners, especially given the Pacific Northwest's relentless rain and moisture. Over time, water exposure can lead to rotting wood, which compromises the structural integrity of the chase, potentially resulting in leaks and further damage to your home. The replacement process begins with a thorough inspection to assess the extent of damage. Any compromised wood is removed and replaced with treated lumber, which is more resistant to moisture and decay<sup>11</sup>. Proper flashing is essential during the replacement to prevent future water intrusion; step flashing is interwoven with shingles to create a seamless barrier against moisture<sup>12</sup>. If you're noticing signs of water damage or suspect your chimney chase framing might be at risk, now is the time to address these issues to protect your home.
