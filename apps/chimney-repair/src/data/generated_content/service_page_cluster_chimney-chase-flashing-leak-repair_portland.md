## Chimney Chase Flashing Repair & Waterproofing
Chimney chase flashing is crucial in protecting your home from water damage, especially in the rainy Pacific Northwest. Proper flashing involves using two layers of water-shedding metal: base flashing and counterflashing. Base flashing includes an apron that covers the downslope face of the chimney, and step-flashing that is interwoven with the shingles along the sides<sup>1</sup>. Counterflashing is then inserted into the mortar joints of the chimney, overlapping the base flashing to create a watertight seal<sup>2</sup>. This layered approach allows independent movement of the flashing pieces, reducing the risk of leaks as the house and chimney settle at different rates. Ensuring that your chimney chase flashing is correctly installed and maintained can prevent costly water damage to your home's structure. If you notice any signs of leaks or suspect that your chimney flashing may be compromised, it’s essential to address the issue promptly. ## Chimney Chase Leak Diagnosis & Repair
Identifying and repairing leaks in your chimney chase is crucial for maintaining the integrity of your home in the rainy Pacific Northwest. A common sign of a leak is dark stains on the ceiling or walls near the chimney, often caused by cracked mortar or missing bricks<sup>3</sup>. To effectively diagnose a leak, a thorough inspection of the chimney flashing, mortar joints, and chimney cap is essential. Look for crumbling mortar, missing or poorly installed flashing, and any cracks in the chimney crown, as these can all allow water to penetrate and cause damage<sup>4</sup>. Repairing these issues involves resealing or replacing damaged flashing, repointing mortar joints, and patching or replacing the chimney cap. Timely repairs are vital to prevent further water damage, which can lead to costly repairs and even structural issues. If you suspect a chimney chase leak, schedule an inspection to address the problem before it escalates.

## Chimney Chase Penetration Leak Repair
Chimney chase penetration leaks can cause significant damage to your home if not quickly addressed. These leaks often occur where the chimney passes through the roof, as water can seep into gaps around the chimney structure. Ensuring proper flashing and sealing is crucial to prevent moisture from entering. The step flashing should be interwoven with shingles to provide a watertight seal, while counterflashing should cover the tops of the step flashing, ensuring water is directed away from the chimney<sup>5</sup>. Regular inspections are vital, as even small cracks or gaps in the masonry or flashing can lead to leaks. Addressing these leaks promptly with appropriate sealants, such as urethane caulk, can prevent the need for more extensive repairs down the line<sup>6</sup>. If you suspect a chimney chase penetration leak, it's crucial to schedule a professional inspection to identify and fix the problem before it worsens.

## Roof Cricket Installation for Chimney Chase
Installing a roof cricket behind your chimney chase is essential for preventing water damage and ensuring proper drainage. In the rainy Pacific Northwest, water can easily pool behind a chimney, creating a dam that leads to leaks and water intrusion<sup>7</sup>. A roof cricket, shaped like a small hip roof, is strategically installed to divert water and snow around the chimney, allowing it to drain efficiently<sup>8</sup>. For chimneys wider than 24 inches, a cricket is not just recommended but necessary to adhere to shingle manufacturers' guidelines and to safeguard your home against water-related issues<sup>8</sup>. Made from durable materials like 20-gauge galvanized steel, the cricket's lower flange is secured with predrilled nails, while its upper edges are sealed with a waterproofing membrane to ensure a watertight fit<sup>7</sup>. ## Chimney Chase Roof Flashing Replacement
Replacing chimney chase roof flashing is a crucial step in preventing leaks and water damage in your Portland home. Over time, flashing materials can deteriorate due to constant exposure to the Pacific Northwest's wet weather, leading to compromised water-tightness and eventual leaks. Proper flashing replacement involves the installation of both base and counterflashing. The base flashing forms the initial barrier, while counterflashing is set into the mortar joints, providing a second layer of protection against water intrusion<sup>9</sup>. The use of high-quality materials like copper instead of aluminum is recommended, as copper is more durable and offers superior long-term performance<sup>10</sup>. During the replacement process, it's important to ensure that the flashing is precisely bent and installed without nails or screws penetrating the structure, as these can create potential entry points for water. By addressing flashing issues proactively, you can avoid the costly repairs associated with water damage to the roof sheathing and interior structures. If you're experiencing leaks or suspect your chimney chase flashing needs attention, schedule a professional inspection and replacement to secure your home against the elements.

## Chimney Chase Step Flashing Repair
Step flashing is an essential component in chimney chase repair, especially in the rainy climate of Portland. It involves interweaving L-shaped pieces of metal with your roof shingles, creating a secure barrier that directs water away from your chimney and roof intersection. This is crucial in preventing water infiltration, which can lead to rot and structural damage<sup>11</sup>. In a proper installation, the step flashing should overlap with shingles by a minimum of 4 inches and be covered by counterflashing, which is set into the chimney mortar<sup>12</sup>. If your chimney chase is experiencing leaks, it may be due to improperly installed or deteriorated step flashing. Replacing this flashing is not just a matter of swapping out old metal; it requires precise installation techniques to ensure longevity and effectiveness. ## Chimney Chase Counter Flashing Repair
Counter flashing is a crucial component of a chimney's defense against water infiltration. In the rainy Pacific Northwest, ensuring that your chimney chase counter flashing is in top condition can prevent costly water damage to your home. Counter flashing is typically installed into the mortar joints of a chimney and overlaps the base flashing to direct water away from the chimney structure. This protective measure is especially vital because it allows the chimney and roof to move independently without compromising the seal, which is important given the settling that can occur in older homes<sup>13</sup>. When counter flashing is not properly installed or maintained, water can seep into the roof structure, leading to rot and decay. Repairing counter flashing requires precision, often involving grinding out the mortar to insert new flashing securely<sup>14</sup>. ## Chimney Chase Roof Tie-In Repair
When it comes to ensuring a watertight seal where your chimney chase meets the roof, a proper roof tie-in is essential. This involves integrating the roof and chimney flashing systems to prevent water infiltration. Step flashing, which consists of L-shaped pieces that overlap roof shingles and run up the chimney sides, plays a crucial role in this integration<sup>15</sup>. It's imperative that these pieces are correctly installed and sealed to direct water away from the chimney structure.
